Gestational Diabetes

What is gestational diabetes?
Gestational diabetes is high blood sugar that develops during pregnancy in pregnant women who have never had high blood sugar before. Blood sugar usually returns to normal after the baby is born.

What causes gestational diabetes?
When you eat, some of your food is broken down into sugar, also called glucose. Sugar travels in your blood to all of your body's cells. Insulin helps sugar move from your blood into your cells. Insulin is a hormone made by the beta cells in your pancreas.

Your cells need sugar for energy. Sugar from food makes your blood sugar levels go up. Sugar from food can be sugar itself, or it can come from carbohydrates that the body turns into sugar. Natural insulin lowers your blood sugar levels by helping sugar move from your blood into your cells.

When you have gestational diabetes, pregnancy hormones that help the baby develop can cause insulin resistance in your body. Your body tries to produce more insulin to handle the extra sugar, but it is still not enough. Sugar stays in your blood instead of moving into your cells, and blood sugar levels get too high.

What are the risks of gestational diabetes?
If blood sugar levels are not managed during pregnancy, this can cause problems for both you and your baby. Your baby gets nutrients, including sugar, from your blood. If your blood sugar levels are high, your baby will get too much sugar. The baby may store the extra sugar as fat, gain too much weight, and become too large.

When you have gestational diabetes, your baby is at higher risk for:
- Injuries during delivery
- Low blood sugar after birth
- Breathing problems
- Jaundice
- Developmental problems, which are not common and are usually related to premature birth
- The possibility of type 2 diabetes later in life

Gestational diabetes may increase your own risk for:
- Type 2 diabetes
- High blood pressure during pregnancy
- Caesarean section
- Gestational diabetes in a future pregnancy

These problems do not have to happen. Managing your blood sugar levels can help reduce the risk.

Managing gestational diabetes
Your goal in managing gestational diabetes is to keep your blood sugar levels as close to normal as possible. Your diabetes care team will help you make a care plan. To help manage your blood sugar, you may need to:
- Eat meals and snacks according to your meal plan
- Get regular physical activity
- Take medicine as prescribed by your doctor
- Check your blood sugar often according to your care plan

What to expect after delivery
Your doctor will check your blood sugar 6 to 12 weeks after the baby is born to make sure it is normal. Because of gestational diabetes, you will be at greater risk of developing diabetes later in life. Your doctor may recommend continued blood sugar checks. You can reduce your risk of gestational diabetes or type 2 diabetes by:
- Continuing to eat a healthy diet
- Getting regular physical activity
- Managing your weight

Talk with your doctor about what you can do to stay healthy, for yourself and your baby.

Understanding Your A1C Test

What is the A1C test?
The A1C is a blood test that tells you what your average blood glucose levels have been for the past two to three months. It measures how much sugar is attached to your red blood cells. If your blood sugar is frequently high, more sugar will be attached to your blood cells. Because you are always making new red blood cells to replace old ones, your A1C changes over time as your blood sugar levels change.

What is eAG?
eAG stands for estimated average glucose and is your estimated average blood sugar. This number translates an A1C result into a number like the one you see when you test your blood sugar at home. For example, an A1C of 7% means your average sugar for the last two to three months was about 154 mg/dL.

What does an A1C/eAG result mean?
Usually, your A1C gives a general trend in your blood sugar that matches your day-to-day checks. Sometimes the A1C result may seem higher or lower than expected because blood sugar was not checked when it was very high or very low.
